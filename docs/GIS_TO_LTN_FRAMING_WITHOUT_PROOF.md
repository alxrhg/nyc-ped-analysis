# From GIS results to “this area warrants LTN-style attention” (without claiming proof)

**Problem:** You want readers to see that the **maps support** focusing LTN-*style* options on Houston–Canal, **without** saying the GIS **proves** an LTN is **necessary** or **will succeed**.

**Solution:** Use a **three-step argument** with **careful verbs**. The GIS establishes **problem clustering**; LTN is a **class of responses** to that *kind* of problem; **whether** to build one is **political and engineering** work your thesis does not complete.

---

## 1. What your GIS actually measures (keep saying this)

- **High** modeled **pedestrian activity** (NYCWalks → tract exposure).  
- **Pedestrian harm pressure** relative to that exposure (NYPD KSI + exposure) and/or **crash density**.  
- **Mixed** residential–commercial fabric (PLUTO).  
- **Transit-rich** context (MTA layers)—*context*, not LTN outcome.

Together these describe **pedestrian-intensive, motor-conflict-prone, mixed-use** urban conditions **in data**, not opinions.

---

## 2. What LTNs are *for* (one sentence you can cite from literature)

LTN-style schemes aim to **reduce through motor traffic** on **local** streets so that **walking, play, and local access** are safer and more pleasant, while **boundary** roads and **service/emergency** access are handled deliberately (UK/planning sources + your Ch. II–III).

So LTNs target a **family of problems**: **too much non-local motor volume** on fine-grain streets where **people on foot** matter—a **conceptual** link, not a regression coefficient from your maps.

---

## 3. The honest bridge (this is the thesis move)

| Step | Claim | Status |
|------|--------|--------|
| A | Houston–Canal tracts **score high** on pedestrian exposure + harm-related metrics + mixed use (vs borough / peers as you define). | **Supported by GIS** (if your tables show it). |
| B | Those conditions match **the problem definition** LTN advocates and planners use. | **Argument** from definitions + lit—not a statistical test. |
| C | Therefore the corridor is a **plausible place** to **discuss** filters, pilots, and **LTN-aligned** street design—not the only place, not a mandate. | **Judgment** you defend in Discussion + qual. |
| D | An LTN **will** work or **should** be adopted without conflict. | **Not** established by your GIS. |

**Formula:**  
**GIS → “problem-salient corridor”** + **LTN → “one policy family aimed at such problems”** → **“reasonable to prioritize design and coalition attention here.”**

That is **not** proof of need in a causal sense; it is **screening + problem framing + democratic/policy next steps**.

---

## 4. Wording that works vs wording to avoid

**Use**

- “**Consistent with** the **rationales** behind low-traffic-neighborhood–style interventions.”  
- “**Concentrates** modeled walking activity and pedestrian harm pressure **relative to** [Manhattan / peer NTAs].”  
- “**Supports treating** the corridor as a **candidate** for further **LTN-aligned** planning and pilot conversation.”  
- “**Necessary but not sufficient** reason to examine filters and boundary-road strategy here.”  
- “**Spatial warrant** for **public** and **agency** attention—not a substitute for traffic study or politics.”

**Avoid**

- “**Proves** the neighborhood **needs** an LTN.”  
- “**Demonstrates** that an LTN **will** reduce crashes / through traffic.”  
- “**Optimal** site” (unless you define “optimal” narrowly as your index).  
- “**The data show** we must implement LTN” (overstates what cross-sectional maps can do).

---

## 5. One paragraph for Chapter V (synthesis) or Discussion

> The tract-level indicators do **not** estimate how motor traffic would **redistribute** under a filter or whether safety would **improve**; they do **not** replace network modeling or a pilot evaluation. They **do** show that, on **open data and a published pedestrian exposure model**, the Houston–Canal study area **clusters** high modeled walking, **meaningful** pedestrian harm pressure relative to exposure, and **mixed** building stock compared to **[borough / peer frame you use]**. Those are the **same dimensions** that LTN-style street management is **intended** to address in dense mixed-use districts. On that basis, the analysis supports **prioritizing** this corridor—not **exclusively**, not **mandatorily**—for **further** technical study, stakeholder process, and, if appropriate, **time-limited pilots** of traffic restraint consistent with New York’s legal and political constraints.

## 6. Where qual and local plans strengthen (not replace) GIS

- **SoHo Broadway Public Realm Plan (2021)** already frames **LTN** and **Holland/tunnel traffic** in the **same geography**—your maps **independently** stress walking/harm/mix; the plan shows **local actors** already argue the **same family** of interventions (`SOURCE_SoHo_Broadway_Public_Realm_Plan_2021.md`).  
- **Interviews/docs** answer **whether** attention translates into **feasible** next steps—GIS does not.
