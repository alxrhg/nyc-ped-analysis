# Using London LTN traffic/outcome data to show “opportunities” (not NYC predictions)

**Goal:** Communicate **what becomes possible** if NYC pursued LTN-style schemes and **serious monitoring**—using **London’s public record** of **reported changes**—**without** claiming those numbers **transfer** to Houston–Canal.

**Rule:** London figures appear **labeled as London** (study, year, metric). NYC map stays **observed NYC conditions** only. Any **bridge** is **prose + caveats**, not a merged “predicted traffic” layer unless you build a **calibrated network model** (out of scope for most BA theses).

---

## 1. What you *can* show (defensible)

### A. **Evidence table (desk research)**

Pull **numeric ranges or point estimates** from **cited** London/TfL/academic sources, with columns such as:

| Source | Scheme / area type | Metric | Direction / magnitude | Interior vs boundary | Time window |
|--------|----------------------|--------|------------------------|----------------------|-------------|
| … | … | Motor volume, speed, injuries, etc. | … | … | … |

**Thesis use:** “**Elsewhere**, evaluated schemes have **reported** [X]. NYC **currently lacks** comparable continuous monitoring on these corridors; **opportunity** = adopt similar **M&E** before/after any pilot.”

You are **displaying opportunities** in the sense of **what policy could measure and what literature suggests is plausible in *some* contexts**—not “this will happen on Broadway.”

### B. **“Evaluation gap” figure (conceptual)**

One panel: **London** — list of **outcome families** often reported (interior traffic, boundary traffic, injuries, mode share, complaints).  
Second panel: **NYC / your pipeline** — what you **can** show from **Open Data + NYCWalks** **today** (cross-section).  
Third panel (optional): **Pilot would add** — counters, camera counts, before–after on treated links.

This **displays opportunity** without fabricating NYC post-LTN volumes.

### C. **Order-of-magnitude band (heavily qualified prose)**

After the table:  
> Published evaluations of **selected** London LTN and related street interventions **sometimes** report **interior** motor volume changes on the order of **[cite range from your table]**; **boundary** roads **sometimes** show **[cite]**. These magnitudes are **context-specific** (network, enforcement, scheme design). **No transfer** to the Houston–Canal polygon is implied; the **opportunity** for New York is to **fund** comparable **before–after** measurement **if** pilots proceed.

Still **not** a map of “NYC traffic after LTN.”

### D. **Side-by-side maps (honest)**

- **Left:** Your **NYC** study area (existing conditions — exposure, crashes, etc.).  
- **Right:** **London** example boundary (one borough or one scheme) **for geographic context only**, with **caption**: “Example of LTN geography in London (for illustration); outcomes from [citation], not applicable to NYC scale.”

Avoid implying the **same** numbers apply to both.

---

## 2. What to **avoid** (reads as fake proof)

- Choropleth or street layer on **NYC** colored by “**predicted** % change from London average.”  
- Single headline: “Traffic **will** drop 40% here” sourced only from London.  
- Treating **one** London study as **universal** without range and boundary effects.

---

## 3. How this connects to your **GIS centerpiece**

1. **Ch. V:** NYC maps = **where** pedestrian–motor tension and mix **concentrate** (your lock).  
2. **Ch. II or VII:** London table + figure = **what has been measured elsewhere** and **what magnitude ranges appear in the literature** for **some** schemes.  
3. **Synthesis:** “The spatial analysis **supports prioritizing attention** to this corridor; London’s **evaluation record** **illustrates** the **kinds of traffic and safety metrics** a serious NYC pilot could **track**—closing the gap between **advocacy** and **monitoring**.”

That is **opportunity** framing, not proof.

## 4. Data you might actually collect (examples — verify before citing)

- TfL / borough **LTN or Streetspace** monitoring summaries (often PDFs or dashboards).  
- Academic papers (e.g. injury or traffic papers on London schemes — read methods and **boundary** vs **interior**).  
- **STATS19** (UK crashes) for **research** that compares areas over time — you cite the **paper’s** aggregates, not necessarily re-run UK GIS unless scoped.

Your **Aldred et al.** PDF is **equity/siting**, not interior traffic counts—pair it with **1–2** TfL or academic **outcome** sources for the traffic column.
