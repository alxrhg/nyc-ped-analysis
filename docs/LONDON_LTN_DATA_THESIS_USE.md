# London LTN data and your thesis — what it helps and what it cannot do

---

## Short answer

**Yes, it can help** — mainly for **international precedent**, **outcome metrics others used**, and **equity / siting** evidence (you already cite Aldred et al. on London).  

**No**, London data **does not prove** that **NYC’s** Houston–Canal **needs** or **will get** the same results. Different city, network, law, and politics.

---

## What London material strengthens

| Thesis piece | How London helps |
|--------------|------------------|
| **Literature / history (Ch. II)** | **Evaluated** schemes (TfL, borough reports, academics): what was measured (injuries, mode share, traffic counts where available), over **what time horizon**, and **controversies** (boundary roads, equity). |
| **Equity / siting** | **Aldred et al.** and follow-on work: **who lives near** LTNs in London — **analogous** argument structure for NYC discussion, not transferable numbers. |
| **Discussion (limits)** | Honest line: “London has **more** post-implementation monitoring in the public record than NYC has for comparable area schemes; NYC work here relies on **cross-sectional** open data until pilots exist.” |
| **Methods reflection** | Table or paragraph: **outcome variables used in London evaluations** vs **what NYC Open Data + NYCWalks** allow you to map **without** a built LTN. |

---

## What London data does **not** substitute for

- **Causal proof for Manhattan:** Effect estimates are **London-specific** (selection into treatment, network, enforcement). You cite them as **precedent**, not as **β_NYC**.  
- **Your GIS centerpiece:** Your **tract maps** stay **NYC evidence**. London is **context**, unless you do a **structured comparative case study** (two cities, parallel questions) — still **not** one pooled “proof.”  
- **“Need” for LTN here:** **Need** remains a **NYC** judgment informed by **your** spatial layer + politics + local plans (e.g. SoHo Broadway vision doc).

---

## Practical sources (desk research; verify URLs and licenses)

- **Peer-reviewed / gray lit:** Aldred et al. (2021) *Journal of Transport Geography*; TfL / borough **LTN or Streetspace** evaluations; UK **STATS19** crash statistics (different schema from NYPD — compare **concepts**, not raw merge).  
- **Policy narrative:** Mayor / borough **LTN programme** documents, consultation responses.  
- **Data portals:** [data.gov.uk](https://www.data.gov.uk/), London Datastore, TfL open data — often **aggregated** or **scheme-level**, not always tract-identical to your NYC workflow.

Use whatever you can **actually cite** and **read**; don’t promise a **London replication** of your pipeline unless you scope a **small, honest** parallel (e.g. one borough, one outcome table).

---

## One thesis sentence

> London provides **documented experience** with LTN **rollout, metrics, and equity debates**; it **informs** how this thesis interprets **LTN rationales** and **evaluation norms**, but it **does not** establish **causal impacts** or **spatial priorities** for the Houston–Canal corridor, which depend on **New York** data and **local** governance.
